Extended planar magnetrons

  1. Magnetron sputtering devices are designed for sputtering electrically conductive targets made of metals, alloys, semiconductor materials in direct current, pulsed unipolar and bipolar, dual mid-frequency sputtering modes. A discharge in a magnetron occurs in crossed electric and magnetic fields.

Magnetrons are widely used for applying the following coatings:

  • metal: Ti, Cr, Zr, Al, in “hot” mode Ni, Co, stainless steel. These coatings are used as adhesive, functional layers in optics, microelectronics, when applying hardening coatings and other areas;
  • semiconductor: Si, Ge, ZnO, ZnS, SiC and other coatings used as functional layers of microelectronics;
  • ceramic: TiN, AlTiN, TixOy, AlTiSiN, TiCN, CrxOy, Al2O3 and many others, used as strengthening, protective and decorative, photocatalytic coatings. (tools, stamps, molds, furniture and door fittings, decorative ceramics).

There are two design versions of the magnetron input

1. A magnetron with a cooling and power supply output on the rear side (on one side in the case of single-circuit cooling, and on two opposite sides in the case of double-circuit cooling Fig. 1) is connected to cooling and power supply using copper tubes in fluoroplastic insulation with sealing units, proprietary design of the magnetron manufacturer.

2. Magnetrons with coaxial input are characterized by the ability to simply change the position in the vacuum chamber by moving and rotating along the axis of the source, or moving the source with a sealing unit to any suitable hole in the chamber. The coaxial input does not require additional electrical insulation and is electrically connected to the installation body and allows for dusting of the target and protection from dusting from other sources by turning the magnetron.

The magnetron may have other design features:

  • with a hollow cooled body (two cooling circuits, anode and body);
  • ion source shutter;
  • ion source rotation device with coaxial input;
  • design features for sputtering expensive materials (gold, silver, zirconium, yttrium, gadolinium, indium alloys);
  • the presence of an option to work with “hot targets” (target temperature up to 900 C);
  • the presence of a built-in gas distribution system or its absence;
  • use in HIPIMS processes;
  • direct and indirect cooling of targets;
  • magnetrons balanced for indium tin oxide sputtering (transparent conductive coatings).
